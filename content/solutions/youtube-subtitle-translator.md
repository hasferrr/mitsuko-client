## The failure mode nobody warns creators about

Many creators translate subtitles by running an English SRT through a generic translator and uploading the result.

It may be technically correct, but it often sounds stiff. The rhythm, jokes, and voice of the channel disappear.

The fix is **tone instructions**: tell the translation what your channel sounds like so it adapts the phrasing instead of copying the English line structure.

## Keeping the creator voice: two real examples

The difference is clearest when you put a literal pass next to a tone-aware one on the same line.

### Example 1 - a mistake anecdote

| Type | Line |
|---|---|
| English creator sample | Here's the mistake that ruined my first export. |
| Literal Indonesian | Ini adalah kesalahan yang merusak ekspor pertama saya. |
| Natural Indonesian subtitle | Ini kesalahan yang bikin ekspor pertamaku gagal total. |

The literal version is grammatically perfect and sounds like a product recall notice.

`Ini adalah kesalahan yang...` is formal textbook Indonesian. No YouTuber talks that way.

The natural subtitle keeps the conversational creator voice: `bikin ... gagal total` ("made ... fail completely"). It works because the tone instructions said this channel is casual and direct.

### Example 2 - a call to action

| Type | Line |
|---|---|
| English creator sample | Stick around, because the last step saves the most time. |
| Literal Indonesian | Tetaplah di sekitar, karena langkah terakhir menghemat waktu paling banyak. |
| Natural Indonesian subtitle | Tonton sampai akhir, karena langkah terakhir paling menghemat waktu. |

"Stick around" is a YouTube-creator idiom, not a physical instruction to stay nearby.

`Tetaplah di sekitar` ("stay around") is the literal version, and it is the kind of line that makes viewers click away.

`Tonton sampai akhir` ("watch to the end") adapts the call to action to wording viewers expect in subtitles. It translates what the line is *doing*, not just what the words say.

1. **Upload subtitles or transcribe audio.** If you already have an SRT, VTT, or ASS file, upload it. If not, [transcribe the audio](/solutions/audio-to-subtitles) first and start from the generated captions.

3. **Export subtitles for publishing.** Download the translated subtitle file and upload it to your video. You keep control of what ships - nothing auto-publishes.

## Why tone instructions are the variable that matters

Generic subtitle translation treats "stick around" the same in a cooking channel, a finance channel, and a gaming channel because it has no idea which one it is in.

Your channel's tone is the context an off-the-shelf translator does not have. It decides whether the localized subtitle feels like *your video* or like a corporate dub.

That is the variable to add: a few lines describing how you talk.

It takes almost no time to write, but it can be the difference between subtitles that grow your international audience and subtitles that make them leave.
