## The contrarian take: AI doesn't replace your localization team

AI subtitle translation should not replace translators and QC editors. For agency work, that is the wrong goal.

The better use is cutting down the first-draft grind: the plain pass where every line gets translated before the real craft begins.

Your team still owns tone, terminology, client rules, QC, and final sign-off. Mitsuko gives them a context-aware draft to start from, not a black-box final delivery.

## What "context-aware" means for an agency file

The difference between a generic machine draft and a usable agency draft is whether the model knew the project rules before it started.

Here is the same Korean drama line handled both ways.

> **Project terminology (user glossary):** `도련님` = Mr. Park, `한강그룹` = Han River Holdings. The speaker is a secretary addressing the heir of a family-owned company.

| Type | Line |
|---|---|
| Korean drama sample line | 도련님, 한강그룹 사람들이 벌써 로비에 와 있습니다. |
| Literal machine translation | Young master, Hangang Group people already to the lobby have come. |
| Context-aware translation (Mitsuko) | Mr. Park, the Han River Holdings representatives are already waiting in the lobby. |

The literal version breaks three things at once. It translates `도련님` to "Young master" instead of the client's glossary entry "Mr. Park."

It also changes `한강그룹` into "Hangang Group" and produces English no human would write.

The context-aware version follows the glossary, uses the role context (secretary to heir), and reads like a line an editor can work with instead of rewrite.

### Series-aware, not just file-aware

| Type | Line |
|---|---|
| Review workflow sample | Previously on episode seven, Rina broke the promise. |
| Single-file translation | Sebelumnya di episode tujuh, Rina melanggar janji. |
| Series-aware subtitle (Mitsuko) | Di episode tujuh sebelumnya, Rina mengingkari janjinya. |

A single-file translator has no memory of how earlier episodes phrased things. A project with episode context and agency instructions does.

So "broke the promise" lands as `mengingkari janjinya`, the series-consistent version, instead of `melanggar janji`, a one-off literal choice an editor would later flag.

The file reaches editorial review already aligned with the series.

## The agency localization flow

1. **Import client subtitle files and project context.** Bring in the subtitle files plus the glossary, character notes, and client style rules as context and custom instructions.
2. **Translate with instructions for terms, tone, and style.** The draft follows your terminology and tone rules instead of guessing. Names, terms, formatting preferences, and client rules come from your instructions.
3. **Review, QC, and deliver final subtitles.** Your translators and QC editors review the draft and own the final sign-off. Mitsuko produces the first pass; the agency produces the deliverable.

## Batch processing for recurring client work

Agencies rarely get one file at a time. You get a season, a course, or a recurring weekly drop.

[Batch projects](/solutions/batch-subtitle-translation) let you load multiple subtitle translations at once with shared context. That keeps terminology stable across files without a human re-checking every term decision by hand.

For recurring client work, that is where the time goes and where the consistency risk lives.
